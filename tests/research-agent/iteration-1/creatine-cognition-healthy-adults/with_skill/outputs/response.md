# Research: Creatine Monohydrate for Cognition in Healthy Adults

**Depth level:** Standard. If you want a citation-heavy deep dive with effect-size tables, ask.

## Claim-Type Audit

| Claim | Type | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| "Creatine improves muscle performance in resistance training" | Strong empirical consensus (hundreds of RCTs) | Very high — not what you asked but important baseline |
| "Creatine improves cognition in healthy adults" | Contested empirical claim | Moderate-low |
| "Creatine improves cognition in sleep-deprived or vegetarian adults" | Empirical (specific populations) | Moderate |
| "Creatine is a nootropic for everyone" | Marketing | Low |
| "EFSA 2024 — no conclusions can be drawn from cognition data" | Regulatory scientific opinion | High |

## Consensus

**Strong consensus (high confidence):**
- Creatine monohydrate is **safe** at 3-5 g/day in healthy adults. One of the most-studied supplements.
- Creatine provides **clear benefit for muscle/strength performance** in resistance training. This is not the cognition claim but is worth flagging as baseline.

**Moderate consensus:**
- Creatine shows **small positive effects on memory** (g ≈ 0.2-0.3) in some meta-analyses, especially in **older adults (60+)** and **vegetarians/vegans** where baseline brain-creatine is lower.
- Creatine shows **moderate positive effects under cognitive stress** — specifically sleep deprivation and mental fatigue.

**Weak / contested:**
- General cognitive enhancement in **healthy, rested, omnivore, young-to-middle-aged adults** — the population you're asking about.
- Effect on **executive function** — no reliable effect in healthy young adults.
- Effect on **overall cognitive function** — meta-analyses disagree.
- **Processing speed / reaction time** — small positive in some pooled analyses, null in others.

## Nuances

### Population matters more than dose

The effect is strongest where baseline brain creatine is lowest:
- **Vegetarians and vegans** → larger effects (Rae et al. 2003 is the canonical citation).
- **Older adults (60+)** → brain creatine declines with age, supplementation shows more benefit (Forbes et al. 2022).
- **Sleep-deprived subjects** → larger acute effects on working memory and attention (McMorris et al.).
- **Healthy, rested, omnivore 30-year-olds** → lowest-effect population. You are here.

This single nuance explains most of the confusion in the public discourse: the "cognitive enhancer" marketing generalizes effects measured in specific vulnerable subgroups to the general healthy population where the data is weakest.

### The EFSA 2024 methodological critique is a big deal

The most-cited positive meta-analysis — **Xu et al. 2024 (Frontiers in Nutrition)** — was formally criticized by EFSA's 2024 scientific opinion for pooling **non-independent cognitive test results** from the same subjects, which artificially inflates the effective sample size and the significance of pooled effects. EFSA concluded "no conclusions can be drawn" from that data.

This is the single most important methodological caveat in the recent literature and is rarely mentioned in the supplement-marketing coverage.

### Dose and timing
- Standard maintenance: 3-5 g/day.
- Loading: 20 g/day for 5-7 days, then 3-5 g. Cognitive trials vary widely on this; results confounded.
- Time-to-effect for brain creatine: 2-4 weeks to saturate. Short trials (<4 weeks) may miss effects.

### Effect size is small even where present
Even the positive meta-analyses find effect sizes in the 0.2-0.3 range (small by Cohen's conventions). For comparison, caffeine on alertness is ~0.4-0.6. Don't expect "noticeably smarter"; expect "possibly a touch better on memory tests."

## Known Pitfalls

1. **Generalizing from vulnerable-population studies to healthy adults.** The biggest mistake in the current marketing wave.
2. **Cherry-picking positive meta-analyses** and ignoring EFSA's methodological rejection of the strongest ones.
3. **Ignoring publication bias.** Many small positive trials, few large well-powered negative ones.
4. **Confusing "statistically significant pooled effect" with "noticeable benefit for me."** Effect sizes are small.
5. **Expecting a nootropic effect from muscle-dose timing/loading regimes** — cognitive-focused dosing studies are fewer and less standardized.
6. **Hype-cycle risk.** Similar pattern to omega-3, curcumin, and ashwagandha cycles — initial positive signal, heavy marketing, later methodological critique, eventual downgrade.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

| Disagreement | Why |
|---|---|
| Xu et al. 2024 meta-analysis: positive on memory, attention, processing speed. | Pooled non-independent results per EFSA; effect may be inflated. |
| Prokopidis et al. 2023 (Nutrition Reviews): small positive effect on memory in healthy adults. | More methodologically careful; effect size small. |
| Sandkuhler et al. 2023 (Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews): equivocal results. | Different inclusion criteria; stricter on study quality. |
| Forbes et al. 2022 (older adults): moderate positive. | Restricted population with lower baseline — more space for benefit. |
| Marketing claims: "proven cognitive enhancer." | Overstates what any of the peer-reviewed meta-analyses support. |

The contradictions are not random — they track population (older/vegetarian/stressed vs healthy/rested/omnivore) and methodological rigor (independent vs pooled outcomes). Once you control for those, the picture is consistent: modest benefit in specific populations, weak evidence in healthy young omnivores.

## Recommended Direction

**For a healthy 30-year-old omnivore with adequate sleep:**

- **Expected cognitive benefit: small-to-zero.** Don't expect a noticeable nootropic effect.
- **If you're already supplementing for training reasons:** any cognitive bonus is a free upside. Keep doing what you're doing.
- **If you're considering starting it purely for cognition:** the evidence is weak for your population. A 3-month self-trial at 3-5 g/day is cheap, low-risk, methodologically weak but reasonable — you'll know if you feel a subjective effect. Don't mistake a self-trial for evidence.
- **If you're vegetarian, sleep-deprived, or over 60:** the case is meaningfully stronger. Worth a properly-run trial.

### Steelman of the pro-cognition view
- Brain creatine is measurable (MRS) and *does* increase with supplementation.
- The mechanism (ATP buffering in neurons under metabolic stress) is plausible.
- The effect in stressed populations is consistent across studies.
- Safety profile is excellent, so low-cost to try.

### Steelman of the skeptical view
- EFSA's 2024 critique of the strongest meta-analysis is serious and rarely acknowledged.
- Publication bias is likely — many small positive trials, few replicated large ones.
- Effect sizes where measured are small (g ≈ 0.2-0.3).
- Healthy rested young adults are the lowest-effect population; they're also the largest market for supplement marketing — structural mismatch.

### Is this a hype cycle?
**Partly yes.** It follows a recognizable pattern: supplement with real but narrow benefits gets generalized into a "cognitive enhancer" for the general population; marketing inflates; methodological critique catches up; eventual recalibration. We're probably mid-cycle on that arc. Safety concerns are not where the issue is — the issue is efficacy being oversold relative to the evidence.

## Further Investigation

- **Primary sources worth reading:**
  - **Prokopidis et al. 2023** (*Nutrition Reviews*) — memory meta-analysis.
  - **Forbes et al. 2022** (*Nutrients*) — older-adult review.
  - **Xu et al. 2024** (*Frontiers in Nutrition*) — the strongest positive meta-analysis.
  - **EFSA 2024 Scientific Opinion on creatine and cognition** — the main methodological rejection of Xu et al.
  - **Rae et al. 2003** — vegetarian population evidence.
- Check **examine.com's creatine page** for a well-curated ongoing summary.
- Assess your own baseline: diet (any red meat?), sleep quality, mental fatigue. These predict whether you'll see any benefit.
- If you self-trial: 3-5 g/day, 8-12 weeks, track with a consistent cognitive task (n-back, dual-n-back, or even simple working-memory test) pre/post. Understand this is not rigorous evidence.

**Confidence summary:**
- "Creatine is safe at 3-5 g/day" → strong.
- "Creatine enhances strength performance" → very strong (but not your question).
- "Creatine benefits cognition in vegetarians / elderly / sleep-deprived" → moderate.
- "Creatine benefits cognition in healthy rested young adults" → weak, contested.
- "Creatine is a reliable cognitive enhancer for the general population" → not supported by the evidence; current marketing overstates what's known.
