# Creatine Monohydrate for Cognition in Healthy Adults

Short version: the evidence for creatine improving cognition in healthy, rested, young adults is weak. It's not the next nootropic hype cycle in the sense of being dangerous or useless — creatine is well-studied and safe — but "cognitive enhancer for everyone" does overstate what the research supports.

## What the meta-analyses actually show

Several recent systematic reviews and meta-analyses exist:

- **Prokopidis et al. 2023** (Nutrition Reviews) on memory: small positive effect in healthy adults.
- **Xu et al. 2024** (Frontiers in Nutrition): positive effects on memory, attention, processing speed.
- **Forbes et al. 2022** on older adults: more substantial benefit.
- **Sandkuhler et al. 2023**: equivocal results.

Effect sizes where positive are small (around g = 0.2-0.3). For comparison, caffeine's alertness effect is larger.

## The critical methodological caveat

EFSA's 2024 scientific opinion on creatine and cognition criticized the most-cited positive meta-analysis (Xu et al. 2024) for pooling non-independent cognitive test results from the same subjects, which inflates the effective sample size. EFSA concluded that no firm conclusions could be drawn from that data. This is a major caveat against the strongest positive result, and it's rarely mentioned in supplement marketing.

## Where the evidence is strongest — and where it's weakest

**Stronger evidence** exists for specific populations where baseline brain creatine is lower or demand is higher:
- Vegetarians and vegans (Rae et al. 2003 is the canonical study).
- Older adults (60+) — brain creatine declines with age.
- Sleep-deprived subjects — larger acute effects on working memory.

**Weaker evidence** for the population you're asking about — a healthy, rested, omnivore 30-year-old. This is the lowest-effect group, and it's also the population the marketing targets most.

## What's supported vs not supported

Supported (moderate confidence):
- Memory improvement in older adults and vegetarians.
- Cognitive benefit during sleep deprivation or mental fatigue.
- Safety at 3-5 g/day.

Not well supported:
- Executive function improvement in healthy young adults.
- General cognitive enhancement in the broad population.
- Reliable "nootropic effect" for rested omnivores.

## Is this a hype cycle?

Partly yes. It follows the usual pattern: supplement with real narrow benefits gets generalized into a broad cognitive enhancer, marketing inflates, methodological critique catches up. The safety case is fine — creatine is one of the most-studied supplements and is safe at normal doses. The efficacy claims for general cognitive enhancement are overstated relative to the evidence.

## Recommendation for a healthy 30-year-old

- **If you already take creatine for training:** the cognitive bonus is a possible small upside; keep going.
- **Starting purely for cognition:** expect a small or zero effect. A 3-month self-trial at 3-5 g/day is cheap and safe, but understand it's not real evidence.
- **If you're vegetarian, sleep-poor, or older:** the case is meaningfully stronger.

## Quick steelman of both sides

**Pro-cognition view:** brain creatine is measurable via MRS, does increase with supplementation, and the mechanism (neuronal ATP buffering) is plausible. Effect in stressed populations is consistent. Safety is excellent.

**Skeptical view:** EFSA's critique of the strongest positive meta-analysis is serious; effect sizes are small; publication bias is likely; the population being marketed to is the lowest-effect one.

## Where to read more

- Prokopidis et al. 2023 in Nutrition Reviews.
- Forbes et al. 2022 on older adults.
- Xu et al. 2024 and the EFSA 2024 response (read both).

**Bottom line:** creatine is safe and probably slightly useful for some subgroups. For a healthy rested young adult, the evidence of meaningful cognitive enhancement is weak and the recent marketing wave overstates what's known.
